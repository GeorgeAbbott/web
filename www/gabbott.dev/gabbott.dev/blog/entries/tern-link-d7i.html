Initial Impressions of the Tern Link D7i

I recently bought myself a Tern Link D7i (granted second-hand), and have since taken it out on a little bit of a ride - I have gotten it for taking it on the train for work. Here is my initial impressions, albeit only after one fairly short (20 minute) bike ride.

General Performance

It is perfectly fine to ride on normal terrain - perhaps it requires a little more effort, and a fair bit more to kick off, but generally, it is not too bad at all. Where the extra effort really shows is uphills - uphill routes that on my normal bike would not tire me out at all properly ache my legs on the Tern, owing likely to the smaller wheels and heavier weight. It's not a major deal, but perhaps this bike is best for flatter terrain, where you are less likely to feel the extra effort you need to put in.

The turning point is interesting - it is a lot sharper (again, I imagine due to the smaller wheels) than a normal bike. It's not like it's so sudden that you will accidentally swerve when riding, but it does take some getting used to. I kind of like this though - once I've gotten used to it, it will mean that I can make much sharper turns, and not need to countersteer into my turns so much.

Folding

I haven't folded it too much yet so will update it with time, but it seems to fold alright. It is a bit bigger than I thought it would be when folded, but likely not an issue.

Internal Gear Hub

Owing to the internal gear hub (the i in D7i) the bike is lovely to switch gears. With a derailleur the other bike gives me all sorts of problems, requires maintenance, sometimes doesn't fully switch gears or requires an extra turn of the crack, etc. On this bike, it seems to just work, which is nice.

Carrying

I might get used to this with time, but carrying this bike is a right pain. It is too heavy to be able to conveniently carry it about. I guess I just need to go to the gym a bit more? But really, this bike is not the easiest to lug about. The good thing is, you can wheel it along the floor when it is folded, even if that is a little awkward (as your hand will be very low).

On the train

I have now carried this on the train to work, and have discovered: yep, it's a lot more inconvenient than I thought. Now, first things first, getting to ride to work is a downright wonderful experience, and, even if my ride to work is only a mile, so about ten minutes to the station.

The folding is quickish, once you get used to it, though it does come apart if you don't wrap the cable hooks around the frame of the bike. To keep it on the train, you really need to do a few extra steps above and beyond the manual: take down the stem/handlebars, bring the saddle down, fold it up as per instructions, but then also wrap the cable ties round (I do it twice, once round the frame and a second round the tyre), latch on the stem using the little hook-strip provided to stop it dangling, and then lock the bike itself to the bar in the train.

Carrying it is painful, so best to try to walk it as much as possible. When folded, you can wheel it, but it is not just difficult because it is so far down (sort of similar to holding a young child's hand, you could get back pain after being hunched over like that for too long), but also, it loses its centre of gravity with a pannier bag attached, and doesn't keep its balance. So best to have it unfolded and wheel it.

Carrying it up and down the stairs at the station was, again, painful. Then also having to carry it up the stairs at my workplace, painful. I ended up folding it again and shoving it under my desk, but it only barely didn't fit when upright, so I had it on its side (which felt a little cruel to the poor machine) shoved far enough under my desk for it not to catch on my feet under the table.

Generally, I notice that, with its 20 in wheels, it is a lot bigger, and therefore more inconvenient, than expected. For instance, getting through doors is a challenge, because the bike is pretty much the width of the door. It means you have to do a very awkward manoeuveur, made all the more unpleasant by the weight of the thing, to be able to get through.

I think putting it under the desk is a bad idea. As said, it's so big it's unwieldy to carry about, especially in human-sized places such as an office. Frankly, it is probably best to find a bike shed and park it there if your work offers one. I can't remember whether mine does - I will ask tomorrow.

Other Quirks

The brakes are a lot stronger than on my other bike, so I have to be careful not to apply too much pressure to the front brake, otherwise I will go arse-over-tit. Also, the chain makes a horrible noise when not pedalling, but maybe that is something that a little TLC can fix. Hopefully it's not permanent...

Talking of going arse-over-tit: I went on a nighttime ride with my dad, where he took my old bike with the lights, and I took the new bike, but didn't have any lights. Well, the results? Predictable! I drove into a pothole I couldn't see, as I had no visibility in front of me at all without the lights (we went down a path without street lights), and off the bike was I. I keep on mentioning the tyre size, but I think with 27 in tyres, the bike could probably have survived that pothole. But mine didn't. Oh well, lesson learnt - though the lesson is probably to just actually use lights, especially when going down a path that makes a darkroom look like midday in comparison.

Overall

Overall, I guess I enjoy it. But beforehand, I think I had a very myopic viewpoint: I will get to ride to work, and ride straight from work to the allotment or to wherever else, and I didn't consider the reality of having to fold and unfold a bike, and lug it about, which is much less glamorous. But the ride is incredible, truly incredible, getting to do a route that I am so used to, but in such incredible speed (well, 6 mph on average I guess) that maybe it makes up for the unsexy stuff around it? Who knows. I will keep on trying it, and see if it gets better. But at present, it's not like its any better or worse than walking, and I could see myself just not being in the mood for the hassle of the bike, and just walking instead. Simpler is sometimes better, I guess.
