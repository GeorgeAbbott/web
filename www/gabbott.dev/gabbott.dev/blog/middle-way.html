The Middle Way

In Buddhist teaching, there is a concept called the middle way, which refers to taking not the path of asceticism nor the path or hedonism, but instead a middle way, a moderate path, neither extremity.

I believe this applies to most things in life. Neither extreme is good; always a middle ground is best.

Below are some particular examples I think exemplify this notion.

Computer Technology

Computer technology has improve over the years dramatically. We have gone from processors which are measured in KHz to MHz and now GHz in less than a century, and even now, our processors aren't just one processor, there's four or six or twelve of them.

As development goes, I believe for example neither to be too ascetic (real programmers use Assembly) nor too high level (only use Python) is good. Instead, a nice middle ground should be found: C, C++, Rust, Zig...

And for software itself: what is best of all is software which is developed to be relatively small: perhaps featureful for its size, but not a giant blob. It is clear when comparing the slow and lumbering Adobe Reader with zathura, or Visual Studio to vim, or similar things. While part of the problem is just inherent to proprietary software, there is also the issues of trying too much. Even just using a GUI over a TUI is hugely inefficient. And sometimes a TUI is worse than a simple CLI. Simpler here is not always better - nobody (well, probably somebody) would prefer ed over vi for instance - but generally the middle ground, here the TUI say, is best. Even then, there are times when a GUI is best, and times when a CLI is best. Right tool for the right job, but often moderate is best between that.

And as for design of programs, maybe some OOP is good. But a SingletonFactoryIocContainerManager or whatever is clearly excessive.

Farming

I saw a while ago a Countryphile episode, where a farmer talked about regenerative farming. In my head an alarm went off, as he used a big word (regenerative) which is from what I can tell generally a sign that whatever is being spoken about is kind of... obvious. And indeed it was. He was talking about not using too much fertiliser or pesticides, or tilling up the soil too much, using a variety of species instead of growing the same F1 hybrid exclusively, or ... basically, don't rape the land. I, even though I don't know much about farming, couldn't help but think that it was all kind of obvious. He was essentially talking about farming the way that farming had been done for thousands of years prior to this. But here it is, presented as revolutionary with big words like regenerative or biodiversity (lots of plants) or biosequestration (don't kill all the trees) or emissions reduction (food that's travelled a thousand miles is worse than local produce), and fancy scientific jargon to complement, when really, it is something we did before we even had modern science to begin with.

Factory farming is the same. The impact of keeping the cow, or goat, or chickens, is minimal. It's even potentially useful: the manure makes good fertiliser for instance. And meat is a useful food source. Putting aside the moral issue of whether it is moral to kill an animal altogether, it seems that the main problem is, as with all things, the scale. We were eating meat for thousands of years without any real issue. Yet only with factory farming has the scale of it begun to bring with it other problems.

Infrastructure

Recently I have been watching a lot and reading a lot about infrastructure, for instance the Not Just Bikes Youtube channel and the Strong Towns write-ups. In it, they stress the importance of incremental, and sustainable infrastructure. For instance, a design emphasising bicycles ("middle technology") is much better than a design emphasising cars ("high technology"). This doesn't mean we can't improve on bikes at all. E-bikes are still much better, though there are problems, e.g. with the sourcing of lithium for the batteries. And cars are sometimes useful (though, if we also have trains and other well invested public transport, not as much).

Small is Beautiful

A fantastic book that is worth reading is Small is Beautiful, by E.F. Schumacher. We do not just need large, hedonist, excess, nor is it good to counter this for small, ascetic, paucity. At times the former is good and at times the latter. More often than not, a sensible middle ground is most desirable. As he says:

What I wish to emphasise is the duality of the human requirement when it comes to the question of size: there is no single answer. For his different purposes man needs many different structures, both small ones and large ones, some exclusive and some comprehensive. Yet people find it most difficult to keep two seemingly opposite necessities of truth in their minds at the same time. They always tend to clamour for a final solution, as if in actual life there could ever be a final solution other than death. For constructive work, the principle task is always the restoration of some kind of balance.
